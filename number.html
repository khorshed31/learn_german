German Numbers — Sample 0 to 12

German Number Pronunciation Guide

This guide shows you how to pronounce any German number using a few simple rules. Read the patterns, then use the Play buttons on this page to check your pronunciation.

1. Sound basics (don’t skip this)
  • z is pronounced like “ts” in cats (e.g. zehn ≈ “tsehn”).
  • w sounds like English “v” (e.g. zwei ≈ “tsvai”).
  • ei is like English “eye” (e.g. eins, drei, zwei).
  • eu / äu sound like English “oy” in “boy” (e.g. neun, euro, neunzig).
  • At the end of many tens, -zig is roughly “tsig” (e.g. zwanzig, neunzig).
2. Core numbers (0–12)

Memorise these first. Everything else is built from them.

  • 0 — Null (nool)
  • 1 — Eins (eye-ns)
  • 2 — Zwei (tsvai)
  • 3 — Drei (drai)
  • 4 — Vier (feer)
  • 5 — Fünf (foonf)
  • 6 — Sechs (zehks)
  • 7 — Sieben (zee-ben)
  • 8 — Acht (akht)
  • 9 — Neun (noyn)
  • 10 — Zehn (tsehn)
  • 12 — Zwölf (tsvölf)
3. Teens (11–19)

From 13–19 you usually say “[base number] + zehn”, written together: drei + zehn → dreizehn, vier + zehn → vierzehn. Two are a bit irregular: sechzehn (not “sechszehn”) and siebzehn (not “siebenzehn”). :contentReference[oaicite:8]{index=8}

  • 11 — Elf
  • 13 — Dreizehn (drai-tsehn)
  • 16 — Sechzehn (zehkh-tsehn)
  • 17 — Siebzehn (zeeb-tsehn)
  • 19 — Neunzehn (noyn-tsehn)
4. Tens (20, 30, 40… 90)

Tens are single words. Learn them once, then reuse them in all bigger numbers. :contentReference[oaicite:9]{index=9}

20 — Zwanzig (tsvahn-tsig)
30 — Dreißig (drai-ssig)
40 — Vierzig (feer-tsig)
50 — Fünfzig (foonf-tsig)
60 — Sechzig (zehks-tsig)
70 — Siebzig (zeeb-tsig)
80 — Achtzig (akht-tsig)
90 — Neunzig (noyn-tsig)
5. 21–99: “unit + und + tens” (reverse of English)

From 21 to 99, German puts the unit first, then und (“and”), then the tens: einundzwanzig = “one-and-twenty” (21), zweiunddreißig = “two-and-thirty” (32). :contentReference[oaicite:10]{index=10}

  • 21 — Einundzwanzig (eye-n-und-tsvahn-tsig)
  • 32 — Zweiunddreißig (tsvai-und-drai-ssig)
  • 47 — Siebenundvierzig (zee-ben-und-feer-tsig)
  • 58 — Achtundfünfzig (akht-und-foonf-tsig)

Recipe: say it in your head as “unit + und + tens”, then hit the Play button on this site to copy the rhythm.

6. Hundreds & thousands

After 100, German just stacks the same pieces: hundert for hundreds, tausend for thousands. :contentReference[oaicite:11]{index=11}

  • 100 — Einhundert (“one hundred”)
  • 101 — Einhunderteins (“one hundred one”)
  • 245 — Zweihundertfünfundvierzig (= zwei·hundert + fünf·und·vierzig)
  • 1 000 — Eintausend (“one thousand”)
  • 1 250 — Eintausendzweihundertfünfzig
